## Limit Definition of Derivative

We will use two well-known formulas for this proof. First, the limit definition of a derivative:

$$
f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h) -f(x)}{h}
$$

We will also use the limit definition of $e$. 

$$
e^x = \lim_{n \to \infty} ( 1 + \frac{1}{n})^{n}
$$

So, let $f(x) = \ln(x)$ and apply the limit definition of a derivative. 

$$
\begin{aligned}
f'(x) &= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{\ln(x+h) -\ln(x)}{h} \\
&= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{\ln(\frac{x+h}{x})}{h}       &\text{From  }\ln(a) - \ln(b) = \ln(\frac{a}{b}) \\
&= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{\ln(1 + \frac{h}{x})}{h} \\
&= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{1}{h}\ln(1 + \frac{h}{x}) \\
&= \lim_{h \to 0} \ln(1 + \frac{h}{x})^{\frac{1}{h}} &a\ln(x) = \ln(x^a)
\end{aligned}
$$

 Let $n = \frac{h}{x}$. Now we have something that looks much more like $e^x$. Notice, that as $\frac{h}{x}$ approaches 0, $n$ approaches $0$ as well. Thus: 


$$
\lim_{h \to 0} \ln(1 + \frac{h}{x})^{\frac{1}{h}} 
= \lim_{n \to 0} \ln(1 + n)^{\frac{1}{nx}} 
$$

Notice that $x$ is constant with respect to the derivative, so 

$$
\ln(1 + n)^{\frac{1}{nx}} = \frac{1}{x}\ln(1 + n)^{\frac{1}{n}}
$$ 

Now we will perform
one more substitution. Let  $m = \frac{1}{n}$, then as $n$ approaches $0$, $m$ approaches $\infty$. So, 
$$
\begin{aligned}
\lim_{n \to 0} \frac{1}{x}\ln(1 + n)^{\frac{1}{n}}
&=  \lim_{m \to \infty} \frac{1}{x}\ln(1 + \frac{1}{m})^{m} \\
\\
&= \frac{1}{x} \lim_{m \to \infty} \ln(1 + \frac{1}{m})^{m}
\end{aligned}
$$

From the properties of the limits of composite functions and the fact that $\ln(x)$ is continuous we have that:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{1}{x} \lim_{m \to \infty} \ln(1 + \frac{1}{m})^{m} &= \frac{1}{x} \ln(\lim_{m \to \infty} (1 + \frac{1}{m})^{m})\\

&= \frac{1}{x} \ln(e)\\
&= \frac{1}{x} (1)\\
&= \frac{1}{x}  \\
&\text{QED}

\end{aligned}
$$

## Implicit Differentiation

Let $y = \ln(x)$. Thus, 

$$
\begin{aligned}
e^y &= e^{ln(x)} \\
 &= x 
 \end{aligned}
 $$
Now  Differentiate Implicitly:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{d}{dx}e^y &= \frac{d}{dx} x \\
y^{'} e^y &= 1   \\
y^{'} &= \frac{1}{e^y} \\
&= \frac{1}{x} \\
&\text{QED}
\end{aligned}
$$
